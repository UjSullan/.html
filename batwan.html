1. Exploring the Internal Profile of Batwan

Batwan (Garcinia binucao) is a sour fruit endemic to the Visayas and parts of Mindanao in the Philippines. It is green when unripe and turns yellowish as it matures. The fruit is round, about the size of a plum, and has a juicy, tart flesh. Closely related to mangosteen, Batwan is a member of the Garcinia family and grows on small to medium-sized trees in tropical climates.

2. Dietary Benefits and Nutritional Values

Batwan is a natural source of vitamin C and antioxidants. Its acidic content aids digestion and enhances appetite. The fruit is also believed to have antimicrobial properties and is used in some traditional medicinal practices to support gut health and reduce inflammation.

3. Cultural Significance

Batwan is a culinary staple in Ilonggo cuisine, particularly in Negros and Iloilo, where it is used to sour dishes such as Kansi (a sour beef soup). It is a treasured regional ingredient that distinguishes local food traditions. Despite being lesser-known in other parts of the Philippines, Batwan is deeply embedded in the food culture of Western Visayas.

4. Economic Contributions

Batwan is typically foraged or cultivated on a small scale, sold in wet markets, and used by home cooks and local eateries. There is potential for commercial use in sauces, condiments, and dried souring agents. Promoting Batwan-based products can help boost agri-tourism and support local economies through value-added production.

5. Sustainability

Encouraging the cultivation and conservation of Batwan supports biodiversity and helps preserve heirloom crop varieties. As a native species, Batwan adapts well to local environments and requires minimal intervention. Sustainable harvesting and propagation contribute to the ecological and cultural resilience of local communities.
