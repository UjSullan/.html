1. Exploring the Internal Profile of Durian

The durian (/ˈdʊəriən/ ⓘ[1]) is the edible fruit of several tree species belonging to the genus Durio. There are 30 recognized species, at least nine of which produce edible fruit.[2] Durio zibethinus, native to Borneo and Sumatra, is the only species available on the international market. It has over 300 named varieties in Thailand and over 200 in Malaysia as of 2021. Other species are sold in their local regions.[2] Known in some regions as the "king of fruits",[3][4] the durian is distinctive for its large size, strong odour, and thorn-covered rind. The fruit can grow as large as 30 cm (12 in) long and 15 cm (6 in) in diameter, and it typically weighs 1 to 3 kg (2 to 7 lb). Its shape ranges from oblong to round, the colour of its husk from green to brown, and its flesh from pale yellow to red, depending on the species. Some people regard the durian as having a pleasantly sweet fragrance, whereas others find the aroma overpowering and unpleasant. The smell evokes reactions ranging from deep appreciation to intense disgust. The persistence of its strong odour, which may linger for several days, has led some hotels and public transportation services in Southeast Asia, such as in Singapore and Bangkok, to ban the fruit. The flesh can be consumed at various stages of ripeness, and it is used to flavour a wide variety of sweet desserts and savoury dishes in Southeast Asian cuisines. The seeds can be eaten when cooked.

2. Dietary Benefits and Nutritional Values

Durian is rich in calories, healthy fats, and carbohydrates, making it an energy-boosting fruit. It also provides a good amount of fiber, potassium, vitamin C, and B-vitamins. Its antioxidants help fight inflammation and promote heart and digestive health.

3. Cultural Significance

In the Philippines, durian holds cultural pride, particularly in Davao City, where it is a symbol of abundance and prosperity. Durian festivals celebrate this unique fruit with parades, delicacies, and tourism events. Despite its strong smell, it is deeply embedded in local traditions and cuisine.

4. Economic Contributions

Durian farming is a major contributor to the economy of southern Mindanao. Export opportunities for fresh fruit, processed products, and even durian-based desserts help generate significant income for local farmers and traders, boosting agricultural livelihoods.

5. Sustainability

Efforts are being made to cultivate durian more sustainably, including organic farming techniques, biodiversity protection, and responsible land use. With increasing demand, sustainable practices are essential for long-term environmental and economic viability.
